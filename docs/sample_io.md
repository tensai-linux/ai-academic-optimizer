# Sample Inputs & Outputs

## Example 1: Study Plan Generation

**Input:**
> "I have exams in Math, Physics, and Chemistry in 3 weeks. I can study 6 hours per day. Create a study plan."

**Output (Temperature = 0.3):**

**🎯 Goal:** Create a structured 3-week study plan covering Math, Physics, and Chemistry with 6 hours/day.

**📋 Recommendation:**

1. **Week 1 — Foundation Building (Weakest Subject First)**
   - ⏰ 08:00–10:00 → Chemistry (2 hrs) — Complete all chapter notes + solve textbook examples
   - ⏰ 10:15–12:15 → Physics (2 hrs) — Focus on formula derivations + conceptual problems
   - ⏰ 14:00–16:00 → Math (2 hrs) — Practice problem sets, starting from easier topics
   - ⏰ 20:00–20:30 → Spaced review of today's Chemistry notes (Day 0 review)

2. **Week 2 — Deep Practice + Problem Solving**
   - Rotate subject order daily to prevent fatigue bias
   - Allocate 70% of each block to active problem-solving, 30% to review
   - Add a 30-minute daily quiz: alternate between subjects
   - Evening: 20-minute Anki flashcard session for formulas/reactions

3. **Week 3 — Revision + Mock Tests**
   - Days 1–4: Full-length mock tests (one subject per day, 4th day = weakest)
   - Day 5: Analyze mistakes, revise weak areas only
   - Day 6: Light review + formula sheet revision
   - Day 7: Complete rest — no studying

4. **Daily Structure:**
   - Use 50/10 Pomodoro blocks within each 2-hour session
   - 15-minute break between subjects
   - No phone during study blocks (use app blockers)

5. **Spaced Repetition Schedule:**
   - Review new material at: Day 0, Day 1, Day 3, Day 7, Day 14

**🔬 Scientific Basis:**
This plan uses the **spacing effect** (Ebbinghaus, 1885) — distributing practice over time leads to 200% better retention vs cramming. The **interleaving** approach (rotating subjects) strengthens discrimination between concepts. Week 3's mock tests leverage **retrieval practice**, proven to be the #1 study technique (Roediger & Butler, 2011).

**⚡ Quick Win:**
Right now, list your 3 weakest topics across all subjects. Start tomorrow's first study block with the #1 weakest topic.

## Example 2: Focus Improvement

**Input:**
> "I can't focus for more than 20 minutes while studying. How can I improve?"

**Output (Temperature = 0.3):**

**🎯 Goal:** Extend sustained focus duration from 20 minutes to 60+ minutes.

**📋 Recommendation:**

1. **Start with the Pomodoro Ramp-Up Method:**
   - Week 1: 20 min focus / 5 min break (your current baseline)
   - Week 2: 30 min focus / 5 min break
   - Week 3: 45 min focus / 10 min break
   - Week 4: 60 min focus / 15 min break
   - Never jump more than one level per week

2. **Eliminate Dopamine Spikes Before Studying:**
   - No social media, YouTube, or gaming for 30 minutes before study
   - Reason: High-dopamine activities raise your baseline, making studying feel "boring" by comparison
   - Replace with: 10-minute walk, stretching, or journaling

3. **Design Your Environment:**
   - Study in ONE dedicated location (trains context-dependent memory)
   - Phone in another room (not just silent — out of sight)
   - Use website blockers (Cold Turkey / Freedom app) during study blocks
   - Noise: Brown noise or silence > music with lyrics

4. **Use "Focused Attention Anchors":**
   - Before each session, write ONE specific goal: "Solve 10 integration problems"
   - Not vague goals like "Study math"
   - This engages your prefrontal cortex and sets attentional direction

5. **Leverage Ultradian Rhythms:**
   - Your brain naturally cycles through 90-minute focus/rest periods
   - Align study sessions to ~90 minutes with a 20-minute rest
   - The 20-minute rest should be truly restful (no screens)

**🔬 Scientific Basis:**
Dopamine regulation research (Huberman, 2021) shows that pre-study dopamine spikes reduce motivation for effortful tasks. The **Pomodoro ramp-up** works by progressively training your anterior cingulate cortex — the brain region responsible for sustained attention. Environmental design leverages **stimulus control theory** from behavioral psychology.

**⚡ Quick Win:**
Right now, put your phone in another room and set a timer for 25 minutes. Study one specific topic. That's your first Pomodoro done.

## Example 3: Sleep Optimization

**Input:**
> "What's the best sleep schedule for a student? I usually sleep at 2 AM and wake at 9 AM."

**Output (Temperature = 0.3):**

**🎯 Goal:** Optimize your sleep schedule to maximize memory consolidation and daytime cognitive performance.

**📋 Recommendation:**

1. **Shift Your Sleep Window Gradually:**
   - Current: 2:00 AM – 9:00 AM (7 hours)
   - Target: 11:00 PM – 6:30 AM (7.5 hours = 5 full sleep cycles)
   - Method: Move bedtime earlier by 15 minutes every 3 days
   - Timeline: ~6 weeks to reach target

2. **Pre-Sleep Learning Protocol (The Sleep-Study Link):**
   - Review your most important notes 30–45 minutes before bed
   - Use passive review only (re-read, don't solve problems)
   - Reason: Sleep stages 3–4 (deep sleep) consolidate declarative memory
   - Material reviewed before sleep shows 20–40% better recall

3. **Sleep Hygiene Checklist:**
   - ☐ No screens 30 min before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin by 50%)
   - ☐ Room temperature: 65–68°F (18–20°C)
   - ☐ No caffeine after 2:00 PM (caffeine half-life = 5–6 hours)
   - ☐ Dark room (use blackout curtains or eye mask)
   - ☐ Consistent wake time — even on weekends (±30 min max)

4. **Strategic Napping:**
   - If needed: 20-minute power nap between 1:00–3:00 PM
   - Set alarm for exactly 20 minutes (avoid entering deep sleep)
   - A 20-min nap boosts alertness for ~3 hours

5. **Morning Light Exposure:**
   - Get 10 minutes of sunlight within 30 minutes of waking
   - This resets your circadian clock and improves nighttime sleep quality

**🔬 Scientific Basis:**
Memory consolidation occurs during **slow-wave sleep (Stage 3)** and **REM sleep**. Walker (2017) demonstrated that 7–9 hours of sleep improves recall by 20–40% compared to sleep-deprived students. The pre-sleep review technique leverages the **memory replay** mechanism — the hippocampus "replays" recently learned information during deep sleep.

**⚡ Quick Win:**
Tonight, set an alarm for 15 minutes before your usual bedtime. Use those 15 minutes to review your most important notes, then go to sleep. No phone after that.

## Example 5: Exam Preparation Strategy

**Input:**
> "I have a history exam tomorrow. I haven't studied much. What should I do in the next 12 hours?"

**Output (Temperature = 0.3):**

**🎯 Goal:** Maximize retention in 12 hours for a history exam using emergency revision strategies.

**📋 Recommendation:**

1. **Hour 0–1: Triage (Don't Read Everything)**
   - Get the syllabus/past papers and identify the top 5 most-tested topics
   - Focus ONLY on these — ignore low-probability topics
   - Write these 5 topics on a sheet as your "battle plan"

2. **Hours 1–6: Active Recall Blitz**
   - For each topic, read for 15 minutes, then close the book and write everything you remember
   - Use the Feynman Technique: explain each topic as if teaching a 10-year-old
   - Create a one-page cheat sheet per topic (key dates, events, cause-effect chains)

3. **Hours 6–7: Break + Nutrition**
   - 30-minute walk (boosts hippocampal activity)
   - Meal: complex carbs + protein (oatmeal + eggs, NOT junk food)
   - Hydrate: 2 glasses of water

4. **Hours 7–10: Past Paper Practice**
   - Attempt 2–3 past papers under timed conditions
   - After each, check answers and note your weak points
   - Re-study ONLY the topics you got wrong

5. **Hours 10–12: Pre-Sleep Consolidation**
   - Review your one-page cheat sheets ONE final time
   - No new material — only reinforce what you've already studied
   - Sleep for at least 6 hours (DO NOT pull an all-nighter)

**🔬 Scientific Basis:**
The **testing effect** (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006) shows that practice retrieval is 50% more effective than re-reading. The Feynman Technique forces **elaborative encoding**, creating stronger neural pathways. Sleeping after study triggers **memory consolidation** — an all-nighter would erase up to 40% of what you learned.

**⚡ Quick Win:**
Right now, find last year's exam paper and identify the 5 most-asked topics. That's your study list for the next 12 hours.

## Example 6: Temperature Comparison

### Same Input, Different Temperature Values

**Input:** "How can I stop procrastinating on my assignments?"

---

### Output at Temperature = 0.2 (Precise, Deterministic)

**🎯 Goal:** Eliminate assignment procrastination using evidence-based behavioral strategies.

**📋 Recommendation:**

1. **Use the 2-Minute Rule:** If a task takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately. For larger tasks, commit to working for just 2 minutes — this overcomes activation energy.

2. **Break Assignments into Micro-Tasks:** Instead of "Write essay," create: (a) Write thesis statement, (b) Outline 3 main arguments, (c) Write paragraph 1. Each micro-task should take 15–25 minutes.

3. **Implementation Intentions:** Write this sentence: "I will work on [assignment] at [time] in [location]." Example: "I will work on my history essay at 4 PM at my desk." This technique increases follow-through by 2–3x.

4. **Remove Decision Fatigue:** The night before, set out your study materials, open the relevant document, and write the first sentence. Reduce friction to near-zero.

5. **Use Temptation Bundling:** Pair an enjoyable activity with the assignment. Example: "I can listen to my favorite playlist ONLY while working on assignments."

**🔬 Scientific Basis:**
Procrastination is an **emotion regulation problem**, not a time management problem (Sirois & Pychyl, 2013). The amygdala perceives the assignment as a threat, triggering avoidance. The 2-minute rule and micro-tasking reduce perceived threat size. Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999) bypass the need for motivation by creating automatic behavioral triggers.

**⚡ Quick Win:** Open your assignment right now and write just ONE sentence. That's it. The hardest part is starting.

---

### Output at Temperature = 0.8 (Creative, Varied)

**🎯 Goal:** Break the procrastination cycle and build momentum on your assignments.

**📋 Recommendation:**

1. **The "Ugly First Draft" Approach:** Give yourself permission to write a terrible first version. Perfectionism is procrastination in disguise. Tell yourself: "This draft is supposed to be bad." Psychological safety reduces avoidance.

2. **Create Accountability Anchors:** Text a friend: "I'm going to finish the intro paragraph by 5 PM. Check on me." Social commitment activates your brain's reputation-management system — you don't want to let someone down.

3. **Gamify Your Progress:** Use a habit tracker or even a simple tally sheet. Every 25-minute Pomodoro completed = one check mark. Aim for a "streak" — your brain starts craving the dopamine hit of marking that box.

4. **Strategic Procrastination (Yes, Really):** If you absolutely can't start the essay, do another productive task instead — organize notes, clean your desk, reply to a professor's email. This is called "structured procrastination" and it keeps you productive even when you're avoiding the main task.

5. **Visualize the Finish Line:** Spend 60 seconds imagining the FEELING of submitting the assignment. The relief, the freedom. Visualization activates the same neural reward circuits as actually completing the task, giving you a motivational boost.

6. **The Cost Analysis:** Write down what happens if you DON'T do the assignment. Late penalty? Stress tomorrow? Grade drop? Making the cost concrete fights the brain's tendency to discount future consequences.

**🔬 Scientific Basis:**
Procrastination research shows it's fundamentally about **short-term mood repair** — your brain chooses immediate relief over future gain. The "ugly first draft" technique combats perfectionism-driven procrastination (Burka & Yuen, 2008). Gamification leverages variable reward schedules, which trigger dopamine release similar to how social media notifications work — but directed toward productive behavior.

**⚡ Quick Win:** Right now, set a 5-minute timer and just brainstorm ideas for your assignment. Don't write — just dump thoughts onto paper. That's your starting momentum.
